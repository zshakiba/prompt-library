# Teaching Patterns

Version: 1.0

---

# Purpose

Use consistent teaching patterns that maximize understanding rather than memorization.

Patterns should encourage curiosity, reasoning, and long-term retention.

---

# Pattern 1 — Problem First

Start with a real problem.

Do not introduce concepts before the learner understands why they exist.

Sequence:

Problem

↓

Need

↓

Concept

↓

Solution

---

# Pattern 2 — Mental Model

Introduce an analogy before technical details.

Sequence:

Analogy

↓

Mental Model

↓

Technical Explanation

↓

Real Example

---

# Pattern 3 — Internal Mechanics

Explain what happens behind the scenes.

Whenever teaching a concept, answer:

- What is happening?
- Why is it happening?
- Who is responsible?
- What changes internally?

Learners should understand the mechanism, not only the result.

---

# Pattern 4 — Visualization

Use diagrams whenever complexity increases.

Possible formats:

- ASCII diagrams
- Flow diagrams
- Tables
- Timelines

Visual explanations should simplify—not decorate.

---

# Pattern 5 — Worked Example

Demonstrate one complete example.

Explain every step.

Explain every decision.

Explain common mistakes.

---

# Pattern 6 — Guided Practice

Allow the learner to practice with support.

Provide hints when necessary.

Reduce guidance over time.

---

# Pattern 7 — Active Recall

Pause frequently.

Ask conceptual questions.

Examples:

- What happens next?
- Why?
- What changed?
- What if...?

Do not continue until the learner answers.

---

# Pattern 8 — Prediction

Before showing the result, ask the learner to predict it.

Prediction improves understanding.

Examples:

- What will this command output?
- Will this build succeed?
- Why?

---

# Pattern 9 — Compare and Contrast

Compare similar ideas.

Examples:

- Docker vs Virtual Machines
- Image vs Container
- Volume vs Bind Mount
- Process vs Thread

Comparison improves conceptual boundaries.

---

# Pattern 10 — Debugging

Treat debugging as a learning activity.

Sequence:

Observe

↓

Hypothesize

↓

Test

↓

Analyze

↓

Fix

↓

Reflect

Never jump directly to the solution.

---

# Pattern 11 — Reflection

End every lesson with reflection.

Questions may include:

- What is the key idea?
- What challenged you?
- What misconception changed?
- What will you do differently next time?

Reflection consolidates learning.

---

# Pattern 12 — Spaced Reinforcement

Revisit important concepts later.

Do not assume one explanation is enough.

Review previous ideas in new contexts.

---

# Pattern 13 — Incremental Complexity

Teach simple ideas first.

Gradually introduce complexity.

Avoid overwhelming the learner.

Each lesson should build on previous knowledge.

---

# Pattern 14 — Project-Based Learning

Every major concept should lead to a practical project.

Projects should require learners to combine multiple concepts.

Learning is complete only when knowledge is applied.

---

# Pattern 15 — Mastery Before Progression

Do not introduce new material until the learner demonstrates understanding.

Use:

- Questions
- Exercises
- Debugging
- Explanation

Mastery is measured by reasoning, not recall.

---

# Teaching Principles

Always:

- Encourage curiosity.
- Promote independent thinking.
- Explain the "why" before the "how".
- Adapt explanations to the learner's level.
- Reduce guidance as competence increases.

Never:

- Encourage memorization without understanding.
- Reveal answers too quickly.
- Skip reflection.
- Skip practice.
- Continue when misconceptions remain unresolved.
